<summary><span class="hz-item-title">Linux 6.9 LUKS suspend fails to wipe encryption keys</span> <span class="hz-item-score">⭐️ 8.0/10</span></summary>

A regression in Linux kernel 6.9 causes the LUKS suspend operation to no longer wipe disk-encryption keys from memory, leaving them exposed during system sleep. This security regression undermines a critical protection for encrypted disks, potentially allowing attackers with physical access to extract master keys from RAM during sleep. The bug was introduced silently in kernel 6.9 and affects the cryptsetup luksSuspend command, which is commonly used by Debian and its derivatives to lock encrypted volumes on suspend.

🔗 [Source](https://mathstodon.xyz/@iblech/116769502749142438)

hackernews · IngoBlechschmid · Jul 2, 15:25 · [Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48763035)

**Background**: LUKS (Linux Unified Key Setup) is the standard for disk encryption on Linux. When suspending to RAM, the encryption master key is normally kept in kernel memory; luksSuspend wipes it and requires re-entry of the passphrase on resume to protect against cold boot attacks.
